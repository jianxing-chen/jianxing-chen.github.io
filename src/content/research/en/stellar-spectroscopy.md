## Background

Understanding the chemical composition and kinematic properties of variable stars in the Galactic bulge is a fundamental challenge in modern astrophysics. The Galactic bulge, being one of the oldest components of the Milky Way, holds critical information about the early formation and subsequent evolution of our Galaxy. Despite decades of photometric surveys, detailed spectroscopic characterization of variable stars in this dense region remains incomplete. This project aims to bridge that gap through a large-scale, multi-epoch spectroscopic campaign using state-of-the-art fiber-fed spectrographs.

## Methodology

Our survey employs medium- to high-resolution spectroscopy (R ~ 5,000–20,000) obtained from both ground-based and space-based facilities. We have developed a custom data reduction pipeline that handles fiber cross-talk correction, sky subtraction in crowded fields, and wavelength calibration with arc lamp exposures. Radial velocities are measured via cross-correlation with synthetic template spectra, and stellar atmospheric parameters (effective temperature, surface gravity, metallicity) are derived using a combination of equivalent-width analysis and full-spectrum fitting techniques. We also incorporate time-series photometry from public surveys to classify the variability types of our targets.

## Current Progress

To date, we have collected spectra for over 15,000 individual stars across 40 bulge fields. Preliminary analysis reveals a bimodal metallicity distribution consistent with previous studies, alongside a newly identified metal-poor tail that may trace an ancient accreted population. We have also confirmed spectroscopic membership and determined orbital parameters for 120 newly discovered eclipsing binary systems. The first data release, including radial velocity catalogs and atmospheric parameters, is currently under preparation.
